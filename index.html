How to Calculate BMI Correctly: A Complete Step-by-Step Guide

DN
Dilshan Nayanajith
📅 January 15, 2025 ⏱ 7 min read 🏥 Health
💪

Body Mass Index — commonly known as BMI — is one of the most widely used screening tools for assessing whether a person's weight is healthy in relation to their height. While it is not a perfect measure of health, understanding BMI can give you a useful starting point for conversations with healthcare professionals and for monitoring your own wellness journey.

In this comprehensive guide, we will walk you through exactly what BMI is, how to calculate it step by step, what the different categories mean, and — just as importantly — what its limitations are.

What Is BMI?

BMI stands for Body Mass Index. It is a simple numerical value calculated from a person's height and weight. The concept was developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s and was later adapted by health organisations worldwide as a quick way to classify people into weight categories.

BMI does not directly measure body fat percentage, muscle mass, or overall health. It simply provides a ratio of weight to height that correlates — on a population level — with body fat levels and certain health risks.

The BMI Formula

There are two versions of the formula depending on the unit system you use:

Metric Formula (kg and cm):

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height (m)²

Imperial Formula (lbs and inches):

BMI = (Weight (lbs) × 703) ÷ Height (inches)²

The key thing to remember when using the metric formula is to convert your height to metres, not centimetres. For example, if you are 175 cm tall, your height in metres is 1.75.

Step-by-Step Calculation Example

Let's work through a concrete example using the metric formula:

  • Weight: 70 kg
  • Height: 175 cm = 1.75 m
  • Step 1: Square the height → 1.75 × 1.75 = 3.0625
  • Step 2: Divide weight by that number → 70 ÷ 3.0625 = 22.86

A BMI of 22.86 falls comfortably in the Normal Weight category — a healthy result.

BMI Categories Explained

The World Health Organisation (WHO) defines the following BMI categories for adults:

BMI RangeCategoryHealth Implication
Below 18.5UnderweightMay indicate malnutrition or other issues
18.5 – 24.9Normal WeightAssociated with good health outcomes
25.0 – 29.9OverweightSlightly elevated risk of some conditions
30.0 and aboveObeseHigher risk of diabetes, heart disease, etc.

BMI for Children and Teenagers

For people under 18, standard BMI categories do not apply. Instead, doctors use BMI-for-age percentiles, which compare a child's BMI to other children of the same age and sex. This accounts for the fact that body composition changes significantly during growth and development.

Important Limitations of BMI

BMI is a useful screening tool, but it is far from a complete picture of health. Here are some key limitations you should be aware of:

  • It does not distinguish muscle from fat. A highly muscular athlete may have a high BMI but very low body fat.
  • It ignores fat distribution. Where fat is stored (e.g., around the abdomen) matters more for health risk than total weight.
  • It varies by ethnicity. Some health organisations recommend lower BMI thresholds for people of Asian descent.
  • It does not account for age or sex. Older adults may carry more fat at lower BMI values, and women generally have higher body fat percentages than men at the same BMI.
"BMI is a tool, not a verdict. Use it alongside other health indicators and always consult a qualified healthcare professional for personalised advice."

Beyond BMI: Other Useful Measures

To get a more complete picture of your health, consider tracking these alongside BMI:

  • Waist circumference — A waist over 94 cm (men) or 80 cm (women) is associated with higher health risk.
  • Waist-to-height ratio — Keep your waist circumference to less than half your height.
  • Body fat percentage — Measured via DEXA scan, bioelectrical impedance, or skinfold calipers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a BMI of 25 considered overweight?

Yes, a BMI of exactly 25 sits at the lower boundary of the "Overweight" category (25.0–29.9). However, it is very close to the normal range and should be interpreted in context with other health indicators.

Can BMI change quickly?

BMI is directly tied to your weight and height. Height changes slowly after adulthood, so any change in BMI primarily reflects weight changes. Meaningful changes to BMI typically take weeks to months of consistent dietary and exercise changes.

What is a healthy BMI for women vs men?

The standard BMI categories are the same for men and women — a range of 18.5 to 24.9 is considered healthy for all adults. However, women typically carry more essential body fat than men at the same BMI, which is normal and healthy.

How to Generate a Strong and Secure Password: The Complete Guide

DN
Dilshan Nayanajith
📅 January 22, 2025 ⏱ 8 min read 🔒 Security
🔐

Every year, billions of user accounts are compromised in data breaches. The most common reason? Weak or reused passwords. Despite years of security warnings, studies consistently show that millions of people still use passwords like "123456", "password", or their own name. If you are using a weak password right now, your accounts are at serious risk.

This guide will show you exactly what makes a password strong, how to generate one, and how to manage multiple strong passwords without losing your mind.

Why Weak Passwords Are So Dangerous

Modern hackers don't just sit at a keyboard guessing passwords manually. They use sophisticated software tools that can attempt billions of password combinations per second. A simple 6-character lowercase password can be cracked in under a second. Here's how quickly passwords can be broken:

Time to crack a password (using modern hardware):

  • 6 characters, lowercase only → Instant
  • 8 characters, mixed case → About 22 minutes
  • 10 characters, mixed + numbers → Several hours
  • 12 characters, mixed + numbers + symbols → 34,000 years

The difference between a 8-character and a 12-character password is not small — it is the difference between your account being cracked in an afternoon versus being effectively uncrackable.

What Makes a Password Truly Strong?

A strong password has four key characteristics:

  • Length: At least 12 characters, ideally 16 or more. Every extra character exponentially increases the time to crack.
  • Complexity: A mix of uppercase letters (A–Z), lowercase letters (a–z), numbers (0–9), and symbols (!@#$%^&*).
  • Randomness: Avoid predictable patterns, words from the dictionary, names, dates, or keyboard sequences like "qwerty".
  • Uniqueness: Never reuse the same password across multiple websites. If one site is breached, attackers will try your password on all other sites.
📢 Advertisement (728×100)

The Four Biggest Password Mistakes

1. Using Personal Information

Your birthday, pet's name, phone number, or favourite sports team are among the first things attackers try. This information is often publicly available on social media and is trivially easy to guess.

2. Reusing Passwords Across Sites

When any one site you use is breached, attackers will test your email and password combination on hundreds of other popular sites. This is called a credential stuffing attack. Using unique passwords for every site completely neutralises this threat.

3. Using Simple Substitutions

Replacing letters with numbers — like "P@ssw0rd" — is so commonly known that modern cracking software has long since included these patterns. Simple substitutions provide almost no extra security.

4. Storing Passwords in Unsafe Places

Writing passwords on sticky notes, in an unencrypted notes app, or in a plain text file on your desktop is a significant security risk. Use a proper password manager instead.

How to Generate a Strong Password

There are two reliable approaches to generating strong passwords:

Method 1: Use a Password Generator

The easiest and most reliable method is to use a dedicated password generator tool. A good generator will create a random, complex password of your chosen length instantly. You simply copy it and store it in a password manager.

Method 2: The Passphrase Method

A passphrase is a sequence of 4–6 random, unrelated words strung together. For example: carpet-volcano-tuesday-river. This is easier to remember than a random string and can be surprisingly strong due to its length. The words must be truly random, not a sentence or phrase from a song or book.

Managing Multiple Strong Passwords

The obvious challenge with using unique, complex passwords for every site is remembering them all. The solution is a password manager — an application that securely stores all your passwords behind one strong master password.

Popular and reputable password managers include Bitwarden (free, open-source), 1Password, and Dashlane. Most browsers also have built-in password managers that are much better than using no manager at all.

With a password manager, you only need to remember one strong master password, and the manager handles everything else — generating, storing, and auto-filling your passwords.

Additional Security: Two-Factor Authentication

Even the strongest password can be compromised through phishing, malware, or data breaches on poorly secured websites. Two-factor authentication (2FA) adds a second layer of security: even if an attacker obtains your password, they also need access to your phone or email to log in.

Enable 2FA on every important account — especially email, banking, and social media. An authenticator app (like Google Authenticator or Authy) is more secure than SMS-based 2FA.

What Is Word Count and Why It Matters for Writers & Marketers

DN
Dilshan Nayanajith
📅 February 3, 2025 ⏱ 6 min read ✍️ Writing
📄

Whether you are writing a blog post, a college essay, a social media caption, or a professional report — word count matters more than most people realise. It affects how your content is received by readers, how it ranks in search engines, and whether it meets the requirements of the context it is written for.

In this guide, we will cover what word count is, why it is important in different contexts, and how to use a word counter tool effectively.

What Exactly Is Word Count?

Word count is simply the number of words in a piece of text. Most writing tools count a "word" as any sequence of characters separated by spaces or punctuation. However, different tools may count things like hyphenated words, numbers, or contractions differently — which is why using a dedicated, consistent word counter is important when precision matters.

Related metrics you'll often see alongside word count include:

  • Character count — The total number of characters, including or excluding spaces.
  • Sentence count — The number of complete sentences, useful for readability analysis.
  • Paragraph count — Useful for checking structural balance in documents.
  • Reading time — An estimated time to read the text, usually calculated at 200–250 words per minute for average adult readers.

Word Count in Different Contexts

Blog Posts and SEO Content

Word count plays a significant role in search engine optimisation (SEO). Studies and analyses of top-ranking content consistently show that longer, well-structured articles tend to rank higher on Google for competitive keywords. This is because longer content has more opportunity to cover a topic comprehensively, include relevant keywords naturally, and earn links from other websites.

General guidelines for blog post length:

  • Short informational posts: 500–800 words
  • Standard blog posts: 1,000–1,500 words
  • In-depth guides (like this one): 1,500–3,000+ words
  • Pillar/cornerstone content: 3,000–10,000+ words

Academic Writing

In academic settings, word count is almost always a strict requirement. Essays, dissertations, and research papers have minimum and maximum word limits that must be followed precisely. Going significantly over or under the word count can result in grade penalties.

Academic word counts typically exclude things like the title page, abstract, table of contents, references, and appendices — so always check the specific requirements of your institution.

Social Media Platforms

Every social media platform has its own character and word limits:

PlatformLimitBest Practice
X (Twitter)280 charactersUnder 240 for engagement
Instagram caption2,200 charactersFirst 125 chars matter most
LinkedIn post3,000 characters1,300–2,000 for best reach
Facebook post63,206 charactersUnder 80 chars for max reach
YouTube description5,000 charactersFirst 200 words are indexed

Copywriting and Marketing

In marketing copy, the guiding principle is "as long as it needs to be, and no longer." Email subject lines should be under 60 characters. Landing page headlines should be concise and punchy. Product descriptions need to balance detail with readability. Knowing your word count helps you trim ruthlessly and ensure every word earns its place.

How to Use a Word Counter Effectively

A good word counter does more than just count words. When using a tool like ToolsHub's Word Counter, here are some practical tips:

  • Check your word count before and after editing to see how much you've cut or added.
  • Use the reading time estimate to gauge whether your content matches the attention span of your target audience.
  • Track sentence count to identify when your writing becomes too dense or too fragmented.
  • Use the paragraph count to ensure consistent structure — especially for long-form articles.

Common Word Count Myths

Myth: Longer articles always rank better

Length alone does not guarantee rankings. A 3,000-word article that is padded, repetitive, and poorly structured will underperform a focused, well-written 1,200-word piece. Quality and relevance always outweigh quantity.

Myth: All words count equally

In SEO, not all words are equal. Keywords, headings, and the first few paragraphs carry more weight than filler text. Focus on making every word purposeful rather than just hitting a target number.

A Beginner's Guide to Unit Conversion: Formulas, Examples & Tips

DN
Dilshan Nayanajith
📅 March 1, 2025 ⏱ 8 min read 📐 Math
⚖️

Unit conversion is one of those skills that sounds simple but trips up millions of people every day — from students doing homework to professionals checking measurements, travellers figuring out distances, or home cooks adapting a foreign recipe. The core challenge is that the world uses two major measurement systems — the metric system and the imperial system — and they don't always talk to each other intuitively.

This guide will walk you through the most important conversions across length, weight, temperature, area, and speed — with practical examples and simple formulas you can actually remember.

The Two Main Measurement Systems

Before diving into specific conversions, it helps to understand the two systems:

  • Metric System (SI): Used by most of the world. Based on multiples of 10, making conversions within the system simple. Units include metres, kilograms, litres, and Celsius.
  • Imperial System: Used primarily in the United States (and partially in the UK and a few other countries). Includes inches, feet, miles, pounds, and Fahrenheit. Conversions within this system are less intuitive.

In international contexts — science, trade, medicine, and aviation — the metric system dominates. However, imperial units remain common in everyday life in the US and in some industries globally.

Length Conversions

Length is the most commonly converted unit type. Here are the key relationships to remember:

Key Length Conversions:

  • 1 inch = 2.54 centimetres
  • 1 foot = 30.48 centimetres = 0.3048 metres
  • 1 mile = 1.609 kilometres
  • 1 metre = 3.281 feet = 39.37 inches
  • 1 kilometre = 0.621 miles

Practical Example: You are travelling in the US and see a road sign saying the next town is 25 miles away. To convert to kilometres: 25 × 1.609 = 40.2 km.

Weight and Mass Conversions

Weight conversions are essential in cooking, fitness, shipping, and science:

Kilograms (kg)Pounds (lb)2.205

Practical Example: A recipe calls for 2 pounds of flour, but your scale only shows grams. 2 lb × 453.6 = 907 grams.

Temperature Conversions

Temperature is arguably the most confusing conversion because it doesn't use simple multiplication. The relationship between Celsius and Fahrenheit involves both multiplication and addition/subtraction:

Celsius to Fahrenheit: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

Fahrenheit to Celsius: °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9

Celsius to Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15

Practical Examples:

  • Body temperature of 37°C → (37 × 9/5) + 32 = 98.6°F
  • Oven temperature of 350°F → (350 − 32) × 5/9 = 176.7°C
  • Freezing point 0°C → 32°F → 273.15 K

A useful mental shortcut: subtract 30 and halve the result for a rough Fahrenheit to Celsius estimate. For example, 70°F → (70-30)/2 = 20°C (actual is 21.1°C — close enough for daily use).

Speed Conversions

Speed conversions come up frequently when reading international news (car speeds, weather reports) or travelling:

  • 1 km/h = 0.6214 mph
  • 1 mph = 1.609 km/h
  • 1 m/s = 3.6 km/h = 2.237 mph
  • 1 knot = 1.852 km/h (used in aviation and sailing)

Practical Example: A speed limit of 100 km/h (common in Europe) = 100 × 0.6214 = 62.1 mph.

  • Handle multiple categories (length, weight, temperature, area, speed)
  • Give instant results without page reloads
  • Allow you to specify both the "from" and "to" units clearly
  • Work on mobile as well as desktop

Common Conversion Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to square or cube units for area and volume. 1 metre = 3.281 feet, but 1 m² ≠ 3.281 ft². You must square the factor: 1 m² = 3.281² = 10.76 ft².
  • Using approximate values for precision work. For scientific or engineering calculations, always use the precise conversion factor rather than a rounded approximation.
  • Confusing mass and weight. Kilograms measure mass; Newtons measure weight (force). For everyday purposes they are used interchangeably, but in physics they are different things.

Many online tools require sign-ups, show intrusive ads, or send your data to remote servers. ToolsHub is different. Everything runs client-side in JavaScript, meaning your data never leaves your device. We also publish in-depth, original articles to help you get more value from our tools.

Our Tools

We currently offer 8 free tools across three categories: Text Tools (Word Counter, Character Counter, Case Converter), Utility Tools (QR Code Generator, Password Generator, Unit Converter), and Calculators (Age Calculator, BMI Calculator). More tools are planned.

Technology

ToolsHub is a static website built with pure HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. It runs on GitHub Pages with zero backend. This means it's fast, always available, and completely free to host.
